## Summary

Yao Yue reflects on the accelerating pace of AI-assisted software development and the resulting identity crisis for engineers. With code generation now producing 100K+ lines from prompts, the traditional tasks of writing and reviewing code feel increasingly obsolete. Rather than accepting the "natural reaction to speed is speed," Yue argues for deliberate slowness as a form of professional integrity and learning.

The core insight draws from neuroscience and pedagogy: mechanical transcription—whether copying chemistry notes by hand or sketching Memcached state diagrams—builds abstract conceptual muscle memory in ways that passive consumption cannot. In a world where machines can generate working systems in hours, the scarcest and most valuable resource is **attention and genuine understanding**.

Yue reframes the engineer's role from producer to curator, but emphasizes that curation requires deep engagement. The post argues that patience is not a retreat from productivity but a commitment to the kind of care and judgment that cannot be offloaded. It is not anti-AI; rather, it asks what becomes worth doing when the machines handle commodity implementation.

## Key Quotes

> "It surely isn't writing code anymore, 99% of the time... Are we agent curators, shepherds, managers? Are we AI psychologists?"

> "While my mind could process the words much faster, taking my time allowed me to absorb the idea much better... the mechanical act of transcribing symbols on physical media is akin to building 'muscle memory', only for abstract concepts."

> "When speed is abundant, patience can become the more valuable provision. Patience means giving a shit—a commitment to true understanding. After all, it is the one thing I cannot completely offload to AI."

> "Time has always been the scarce resource. Now more than ever, my values are measured by what I am willing to slow down for."

## Takeaways

- **Role redefinition required**: Traditional engineering duties (coding, code review) are being absorbed by AI; the question of what engineers do next is open and urgent.
- **Slowness as epistemic practice**: Deep understanding cannot be rushed or outsourced; deliberate engagement with problem space is a learnable and defensible skill.
- **Judgment over production**: The post frames curation, decision-making, and care as the non-offloadable core of engineering—but these require genuine investment of cognitive time.
- **Values signaled by patience**: In abundance, scarcity shifts. Attention and depth become markers of professional integrity and personal values.
- **Wandering and wondering**: The post advocates for a kind of exploratory, embodied thinking ("knocking on loose tiles") rather than direct optimization.

## Open Questions

- How do teams actually measure or train for the kind of deep understanding Yue advocates? Is it teachable, or is it primarily a matter of individual discipline?
- Does this apply equally to all engineering domains, or is deep engagement more critical in certain contexts (e.g., systems design vs. CRUD API work)?
- How do we scale organizations where the bottleneck is human patience and care rather than code throughput?
- Is there a risk that "sit with it" becomes a justification for slow delivery, rather than a genuine epistemological stance?
