## Summary

Jamie Hurst, a senior software engineer three years into heavy AI adoption at his organization, reports a counterintuitive outcome: AI has expanded senior roles rather than eliminated them, but in ways that are creating organizational and personal sustainability problems.

Key pattern: the cost of building has collapsed (proposal → PoC → demo → ship now takes weeks instead of months), but organizational alignment cost has risen. When multiple teams can independently build solutions to the same problem faster than before, coordination and consolidation become harder, not easier. This advantage accrues to individuals who adopt AI tools effectively, creating a visible skills redistribution that most organizations don't discuss openly.

The senior role paradox: Hurst codes *more* now (most days) than before, despite expectations that seniority should move toward pure strategy. The reason: seniority is where system-level understanding lives, so senior engineers are positioned to recognize where AI applies across the SDLC, write proposals, navigate organizations, *and* build the thing themselves. The work that used to need a team sometimes needs one person.

The unsustainability comes from competing expansion: more hands-on engineering *and* more strategic writing *and* more meetings as the SME on GenAI. The week is the same length. What gave way: mentoring (1-2-1s), thinking time, and unstructured slack. These human-focused and reflective work items don't benefit from AI tooling and can't be backloaded, so they compress first. Hurst has made explicit choices to cut mentoring under pressure.

The productivity narrative says "we ship more because we're more productive." The lived experience: "we ship more because expectations expanded by more than the productivity gain, and the gap is being made up in hours, attention, and the parts of the role that weren't supposed to be optional."

Hurst concludes the role at this pace is not sustainable, despite genuine motivation and impact. Thinking time—the work senior roles are supposed to be built around—now happens on holiday.

## Verbatim quotes

> The biggest change in how I work is the collapse of the gap between idea and demoable thing.

> The cost of building has collapsed, but the cost of aligning organisationally has not. If anything, it's gone up.

> The bias to action is genuine, but it isn't neutral. The engineers who've adopted these tools effectively get heard more often, get their proposals taken seriously more often, and shape direction more than those who haven't.

> AI landed on senior roles earlier than it landed on junior ones... The work that used to need a team now sometimes needs one person with the right tools, and that person tends to be senior because seniority is where the system-level understanding lives.

> The 1-2-1 work doesn't benefit from AI tooling. You can't backload it. It requires dedicated time and attention, and when the other parts of the role are expanding to consume the available hours, the mentoring is the first thing to go.

> The productivity gains from AI got captured by output volume rather than output quality. The org's expectations rose to absorb the speed-up, and the slack that used to exist between tasks, the unstructured time where strategic thinking actually happens, got eaten first because it's invisible on a dashboard.

## Takeaways

- **Senior roles absorb new tools first and earliest**: not to become pure strategists, but to become force multipliers who understand systems deeply enough to wield AI across the SDLC. This concentrates power and visibility in already-senior people.

- **Alignment and coordination are the new bottlenecks**: when building is cheap, preventing duplicate solutions and ensuring organizational coherence becomes the hard problem. This work is invisible, non-compressible, and gets squeezed.

- **Mentorship and thinking time are structural casualties**: work that requires sustained attention and can't be parallelized or backloaded gets cut first under velocity pressure, even though it's supposed to be core to senior roles.

- **Productivity gains are captured by expectations, not relief**: organizations expand expectations to match new capability. Individual sustainability depends on negotiating scope protection, which most senior engineers don't do explicitly.

- **Skills redistribution inside orgs happens without governance**: individuals who adopt tools effectively gain disproportionate influence and are heard more often. This is a real but mostly undiscussed power shift.

## Open questions

- What does sustainable senior-level AI adoption look like? Is it possible without capping expectations?
- How do organizations measure and account for alignment/coordination work that's now critical but invisible?
- Do orgs need different role shapes or headcount models to preserve mentorship and thinking time?
- What happens to junior engineer development if mentorship is systematically cut at scale?
- Is the bias toward action and visibility (favoring fast-moving tool adopters) a feature or a bug?
