## Summary

An industry critique from David Curlewis (shared by Annie Vella) on the problematic substitution of outcome measurement with volume metrics in AI productivity claims. The post argues that "percent of code written by AI" is fundamentally a vanity metric—"just lines of code with a better publicist"—and that real measurement requires harder leadership work: clarifying actual desired outcomes, talking directly to practitioners, and running incremental improvement cycles (PDSA).

Vella's amplification emphasizes the management discipline required: acknowledging that truly important metrics may be unknowable (citing Deming/Nelson), but necessarily accounting for them anyway. She reframes this as a signal-detection problem: engineering teams can articulate what works and doesn't work in their AI adoption; leadership must listen rather than dismiss qualitative feedback as noise.

## Key claims

- Volume metrics (lines of code, AI-written percentage) have replaced actual outcome claims in industry discourse
- Research supports this critique (referenced but not detailed in the post)
- The work required is clarifying *what you're actually optimizing for* — often left implicit
- Direct signals from practitioners (engineers) are reliable; PDSA cycles operationalize good parts incrementally
- Deming's principle: successful management must account for figures that are "unknown or unknowable"

## Verbatim quotes

> "Percent of code written by AI" is just lines of code with a better publicist.

> "You can't just throw your hands in the air and settle for measuring things we've all known for years aren't good measures... Creating clarity around what you're really aiming for might be the very best thing you could do right now."

> "If you want to know how your AI transformation is going, at least within software engineering, *talk to your engineers*. Ask them to share how it's actually working, or not. Dig into what feels good about it and what doesn't."

> "The most important figures that one needs for management are unknown or unknowable, but successful management must nevertheless take account of them." (Deming, citing Lloyd S. Nelson)

## Takeaways

- **Metrics arbitrage risk**: Easy-to-measure activity metrics (lines of code, commit frequency, tool adoption %) drive decision-making when harder outcome metrics aren't defined. This is a leadership gap, not a measurement gap.
- **Practitioner signal is real data**: Engineers' lived experience of whether AI tooling improves or impedes their work is not anecdote; it's actionable signal for incremental improvement.
- **PDSA over top-down KPI**: Running small cycles (plan-do-study-act) with engineering teams to isolate what works, operationalize it, and repeat is more robust than declaring org-wide AI productivity metrics at the start.
- **Outcome clarity is primary work**: Before picking any metric, leadership must explicitly answer: what are we trying to optimize? (Quality? Throughput? Time-to-market? Developer satisfaction? Risk reduction?) Without this, any metric is decorative.
- **Unknowability is okay**: Accepting that the most important effects may not be easily quantifiable, yet still building feedback loops to detect them, is mature management in transformative change.
